Reading: Rune Madsen, Programming Design Systems

Meta-design is much more difficult than design; it is easier to draw something than to explain how to draw it. […]However, once we have successfully explained how to draw something in a sufficiently general manner, the same explanation will work for related shapes, in different circumstances; so the time spent in formulating a precise explanation turns out to be worth it

This quote speaks to my visual design practice so well, as we are actually creating our own rules and formula for our own design. In opposed to freehanded design, creating design system actively involves mathematics allow us to think ahead of what are the possibilities can be generated through pre-defined visual elements. I have always been interested in experimenting design system and expand the visual design through . Finally, this piece of writing really provides me some good text references to learn the theory of design system. Building your own design tools

“In visual perception a color is almost never seen as it really is - as it physically is. This fact makes color the most relative medium in art.” Rather than beginning a fruitless search for such divine proportions to serve a gross simplification of the world, we will instead focus on a more concrete history of how technology long has inspired designers to use geometric constraints to ease the burden of their work.

When we are first exposed to art and designed, we are often urged to study the color theory and design rules as they are the bible of what respectable art and design should be. As it turned out, the rules are not science. Rather, they are just some arbitrary result of our ancestor's experimentation. Knowing this also emancipates the designers from having to abide to strict conventions as now I feel like I can be more confident and bold of my own experimentation on the rules of visual design.

“Anyone willing to take the necessary trouble will find that, with the aid of the grid system, he is better fitted to find a solution to his design problems which is functional, logical and also more aesthetically pleasing”
One can imagine how the critics at the time must have sounded a lot like what we hear today about the computer: That these new machines constrain the artist and make the design products uniform.

I cant agree with the statement more as I understand the importance of the rules as I use them everyday in my own design. As much as I love using a design system, I still question what are the implications of the rules themselves. The moment the rules are established, it also means certain things are eliminated or forced to conform the universality of the design itself. It may not be entirely relevant, but I recently encountered an article that talks about the qwerty keyboard has impaired Chinese speaking population's ability to write Chinese characters, since the keyboard systems only suggest the phonetics of Chinese and does not require the user to know how to write a character. QWERTY is the invention of the west and it has become the standard and rules of the computer. Therefore, when we try to include other languages and culture into the standardized system, a different culture has to force itself to fit in the system and we sometimes cannot foresee the ramifications of it.

Reading: Chapter 7 "Unfortunate Shortcuts" from Janelle Shane, You Look Like a Thing and I Love You

“The way to detect overfitting is to test the model against data and situations it hasn’t seen. ”

This quote not only applies to training of AI but also the design process. A lot of design that goes out of hand after they are released into the world because they were not tested in a situation hasn't seen. But this also leads to another question, so how do we envisage those scenarios without dropping the AI/product to the unknown territory. How do we foresee the unexpected? Is there a way to solve those problems rapidly once it occurs in real life?

“AIs give us exactly what we ask for, and we have to be very careful what we ask for. ”

Not only do we have to be super careful about what we are asking for, I think we also have to recognize the fact that whatever we feed into the system is mostly biased due to our own rigid perception of the world. If the thing we ask is rigid, then the feedback we get from the computer is going to be rigid too.

“They’re not telling us what the best decision would be—they’re just learning to predict human behavior”

I often forgot computers are actually not as smart as we want them to be. They can only process past incidents and make predictions based on past experience. They don't know how to make something new and they definitely will not tell us the best decisions. I often wonder if there is a way to break the algorithm on Spotify. At first, Spotify seems to recommend me the music that I will really like. However, now it kinda repeats itself and keep suggesting the same music I do not enjoy repeatedly. It does not know what I like and it can only predict what I may like. At the end, I have to go out of my way to find the music that I will enjoy rather than letting the machine decides it for me.

Reading: Joyce Carol Oates, Notes on Failure

The spectre of failure hunts us less than the spectre of failing — the process, the activity, the absorbing delusionary stratagems. The battle lost, in retrospect, is after all, a battle necessarily lost to time: and, won or lost, it belongs to another person.

From my understanding, this quote is describing that we are more entrapped in the feeling of failing rather than the dread for failure, and the battle of ideating and creating is ultimately lost to time. I often find myself struggling in creative process not solely because I fear that the work could possibly be a failure but more or so I am distressed that I will waste all this time, effort and agony to a massive failure. This quote really hits home and it vividly captures my existential dread. My life goes on regardless of my failure, but all the time I lost will never come back and I only have so much time.

Assuming that all art is a metaphor, or metaphorical? — what really is the motive for metaphor?—is there a motive?—or, in fact, metaphor?—can one say anything finally, with unqualified confidence, about any work of art?—why it strikes a profound, irresistible, and occasionally life-altering response in some individuals, yet means very little to others ... we fall in love with certain works of art, as we fall in love with certain individuals, for no very clear motive.

I guess the word niche could be a good fit to describe how the writer says about the metaphor of art and the motive for the metaphor. As a creative individual, we are all obsessed with certain ideas (I use the word idea but the idea can refers to literally anything). We can absolutely articulate all the reasons why we are so tantalized by them, however, the reasons may mean nothing to others. Maybe that is why we feel so lonely sometimes as if there is no one in the world could share the same amount of excitement to certain things like we have.

A study should really be done of artists' private systems — that cluster of stratagem, both voluntary and involuntary, that make daily life navigable. here we could find, I thhink, a bizzare and ingenious assortment of Great Religions in embryo—a system of checks and balances, rewards, and taboos, fastidious as a work of art.

I really wish someone can do a study of that. I have a secret obsession that I often go on the website Nowness where they have a collection of videos that they visit artists' home. Not only am I marveled by the astounding architecture, but I am even more captivated by how these artists design an optimal living/creative space for their mind and their life. How do they organize their home? What are the ornamentations? What kind of furniture do they have? Is there any clutter? How big/small is the space? Do they display their own work? There is so much you can dissect by looking at the home that they carefully curated for themselves.

Chapter 2 "A Brief Visit to the Systems Zoo" from Thinking in Systems by Donella H. Meadows

Because it means there will always be delays in responding. It says that a flow can’t react instantly to a flow. It can react only to a change in a stock, and only after a slight delay to register the incoming information.

Whenever we are trying to develop a system, we first make a draft how system would work based on specific theories and models that we are looking for. However, since the entire structure could easily be overthrown by latency and discrepancy in the flow of the system, I really wonder if there is an efficient way to identify the source of discrepancy in larger and even more complex structures or is there some kind of "activity monitor" for systems to inspect the flows of the system?

One of the central insights of systems theory, as central as the observation that systems largely cause their own behavior, is that systems with similar feedback structures produce similar dynamic behaviors, even if the outward appearance of these systems is completely dissimilar.One of the central insights of systems theory, as central as the observation that systems largely cause their own behavior, is that systems with similar feedback structures produce similar dynamic behaviors, even if the outward appearance of these systems is completely dissimilar.

Even through the examples given in the book we can see how the model of car sales is similar to the water sewage of the bath tub, which I found quite fascinating and wonder if I can identify the application of that model in other usage in our everyday life as well. It is quite interesting to see that how the same model could be apply to something so big like the population system and something comparatively minor like the heating system in our house. I wish I can see more examples of these models.

I will just point out that, according to the dynamics of depletion, the larger the stock of initial resources, the more new discoveries, the longer the growth loops elude the control loops, and the higher the capital stock and its extraction rate grow, and the earlier, faster, and farther will be the economic fall on the back side of the production peak. Unless, perhaps, the economy can learn to operate entirely from renewable resources.

This probably comes out more of a rant rather a direct response to the text. Since we clearly understand the fact that our economy model is built based on finite resources and the dynamics of depletion based on the model, I really wonder why we are not tackling the future problems that stem from our current model and take little measures to counter the future fall back of the production peak. Is it simply about the instant gratification and dismissing the future ramification? Are we really that shorttsighted?

Chapter 3, "Design as the Machines Come to Life" from Synthetic Aesthtics

In art and design, I use the "experiment" as an open-ended process to open up and reveal potential ideas; in science, the "experiment" is a tool to generate data to test a hypothesis. Repeating and achieving the same result is key to scientific method, whereas the experiment process in art often seeks out the exceptional or unique. (P.40)

I started contemplate on a lot of terminologies after I learned more about critical theories. A term could have multiple meanings if we approach it from different field of studies and serve as an entirely different signifier in different context. I found this quote interesting because it also suggest how a designer perceives design and experiment is not the way biologists perceive design and experiment. Does that mean bio-design is not design? And how do we think about bio-design critically through the framework of design and biology.

how much would a biological designer need to know about biology to design it well? I have been wondering about this question since I arrived at the MDP. We make so much work that requires comprehensive research on specific topics especially when it comes to projects that deals with science, technology and speculative future (which is almost everything that we do at MDP). Just like earlier in the chapter that Ginsberg mentioned about deskilling for adaptability, deskilling is often widely adopted when it comes to learning about technology, for example visual coding softwares. I understand that we could not be the expert in every single field that we are interested but I also wonder how much do we need to know in order to have credible, sensible opinions on certain topics? How much do I need to know about technology so I can tell people that I work with creative technology? The accessibility to specific expertise often comes with compromises of the depth of knowledge.

Are we playing the role of god and deciding the lifespan of the product/life form? Are we creating lives soley for our own consumption in order create the illusion of unlimited resources?

"Ruined by Design" by Mike Monteiro: "Moving Fast and Breaking Things", "All the White Boys in the Room", "Choosing Where to Work", "How to Set up for Success" and "Oh, the Monsters We'll Kill".

“There’s no longer room in Silicon Valley to ask why. Companies task designers to move fast and break things. How has become more important than why. How fast can we make this? How can we grab the most market share? How can we beat our competitors to market? ”

Designers are sometimes treated like machines, and we are just tools to make things beautiful and efficient. I know most of the jobs are paid by hours but for design it is especially so. When we are making a design project people pay us by hours and they are always wondering where did their money go. Speaking from my own experience working in a design studio. I was often asked not to spend too much time on conceptualizing the design because we have to manually log how much time we spend on specific tasks. The idea of how time plays a role in creative process.But I also relate this back to a conversation we had about the most original ideas. Even though everything is a remix of a previous creation, we are still competing to release the idea before our competitors. If we are not fast enough someone else will release a similar design before us andd we will not be able to claim to have the most original ideas.

“My city is a fucking mess. My country is a fucking mess. The internet is a fucking mess. But in none of those cases is the whole answer to look for efficiencies. We need to celebrate the differences. ”

I see many designers dread messiness. We always want things that are simple, clean and efficient, and the opposite often seen as unprofessional and undesirable. I sometimes concern people will dismiss my work because the style is too messy and not slick. It seems like people dont have time to read into the implication of those design choices. Everything has to fit into the box, the template, the existed system.

“I believe that our job as designers, and as human beings, is to use our skills to protect the voices that are most in need of protection. When you use your voice to question someone else’s humanity, you forfeit yours.”

I think that is the most disconcerting implication of designs. The people who are going to be affected by the products are usually not included in the design process. Very often, outreaching potential users are labor intensive, time consuming and economically inefficient, and it just doesn't seem "worth it" to include that in the production. On the other hand, I agree with the writer that we should stand up for the people who are going to be negatively impacted by the design. However, I think if those who are impacted are included in the process, the designers do not have to play the roles of hero who voice for them as the evidence will speak for themselves.
